Na asato vidyate bhavo

Seeker

Perception of Darkness:

  • Darkness is defined as that which obstructs the function of the eyes, according to shashtras.
  • Since darkness hinders the eyesight, we do not perceive objects in darkness.
  • In Sanskrit, darkness is termed "andhakāra," meaning the factor that makes the eyes blind.
  • Consequently, when we claim to experience darkness, we are actually experiencing the blindness caused by darkness, not darkness itself.

Na asato vidyate bhavo:

  • Object and existence are often experienced together, leading us to perceive them as one entity.
  • This misunderstanding arises due to "sāmānādhikaraṇyam," where both object (mithyā) and existence (satyam) are experienced simultaneously.
  • An example is provided where the experience of a hand is treated as a single entity, although it comprises both the hand (object) and the light falling on it (existence).
  • This composite experience results in overlooking the distinction between object and existence.

Invariable and Variable Existence:

  • Object experience varies, indicating the variability of the object itself, termed "asat" or unreal.
  • Conversely, existence experience remains constant, implying the invariable nature of existence, termed "sat" or real.
  • The object's variability is demonstrated through changes in perception, while existence remains consistent despite changes in objects.
  • Even in the absence of objects, existence persists, demonstrating its invariability and reality.

Attributive Existence:

  • Existence experienced is attributed to objects and is always associated with them.
  • When objects cease to exist, the attributive existence shifts to other objects, maintaining its continuity.
  • The pure existence, not dependent on objects, remains unmanifest and cannot be directly experienced.
  • The presence of existence is constant, whether manifest or unmanifest, leading to the recognition of its eternal nature, termed "satyam."

Conclusion:

  • Existence is not a property of objects but an independent principle pervading them.
  • It transcends the boundaries of objects and continues to exist beyond their dissolution.
  • Pure existence, unaffected by the presence or absence of objects, is termed "brahman" and recognized as "satyam" due to its inherent and unchanging nature.

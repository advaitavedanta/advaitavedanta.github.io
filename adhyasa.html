    adhyAsa

    All these mistakes are called adhyasa or superimposition.

    Shankara explains it simply as atasmin tadbuddhi -  "thinking 'this thing' is 'that thing' when it's not 'that thing at all'...

    Or, seeing something completely different in a locus where some thing else is present"."

    For example, when we see someone's short body and say "I am short," we're putting the body's quality onto ourselves.

    Also, when we say "I am happy or sad," we're putting the mind's feelings onto ourselves. That's called dharma adhyasa.

    Imagine two people with similar noses; we might think one person's qualities belong to the other, even without knowing them. That's dharmi adhyasa. (the thief superimposed on the pillar).

    Then there's shobhana adhyasa,  means superimposition of beauty or goodness over some thing. According to this concept, before we ever like or try to have an object, this is what we do. Unknown to ourselves- we discover some reason, a good reason, for us to have or enjoy or to be associated with that object. The physical manifestation of this can be observed in all walks of life. Each of us consciously or unconsciously tries to improve one’s appeal by becoming more beautiful/ handsome. Empirical studies carried out indifferent countries, across different cultures/ societies have mostly led to following observations: 

    • Products and services sell better if famous people endorse them.
    • Lots of ads use images of beautiful or strong people to make products seem more appealing.
    • Women often do well in jobs where they have to talk to people or sell things.
    • Good-looking people sometimes get treated better in schools or at work, even if they're not doing anything special.
    • And sometimes, good-looking kids might get into less trouble for the same mistakes at home or school.

    There's gnyana adhyasa, like mistaking the sunrise for the sun moving.
    There's arthAdhyAsa, like mistaking a rope for a snake.

    (Perceptually correct factually wrongs - gyaanaadhyaasa)
    (Object is mistaken for another object - arthaadhyaasa)

    All these are mistakes. When we understand what's really going on, the mistakes disappear, and we see things as they are.

    kAryAdhyAsa and kAraNAdhyAsa -  The superimposition of primal ignorance
    which is jagat upAdAna kAraNa on brahman is kAraNAdhyAsa.
    The superimposition of the world on Brahman due to that primal ignorance is
    kAryAdhyAsa.

    svarUpAdhyAsa and samsargAdhyAsa- the superimposition of the svarUpa /
    the thing-itself on another (a snake on a rope) is svarUpAdhyAsa, whereas the superimposition of the attributes of a thing on another is samsargAdhyAsa eg, the redness of a flower superimposed on crystal.

    nirupAdhika and sopAdhika, adhyAsa involve the presence or absence of an "upAdhi," which transfers its attributes to something nearby. In nirupAdhika adhyAsa, the substratum (like a rope) is misunderstood or not clearly perceived, leading to mistaken identities (like mistaking the rope for a snake). This misconception disappears with the knowledge of the substratum.
    In sopAdhika adhyAsa, an upAdhi (like a nearby flower) is present, causing the mistaken perception (like seeing a crystal as red). Even if we know the crystal is colorless, as long as the flower is nearby, the mistaken perception persists.


    In Vedanta, understanding mostly involves gnyana adhyasa. We're in the dark about things, so knowledge is the light.

Vedanta

Samaanaadhikaranya 

Sarvam khalu idam brahm. Words idam and sarvam are in samaanaadhikaranya, in apposition, revealing the same object. Devadatta is a grammarian, where devadatta and grammarian are in samaanaadhikaranya, revealing one and same person. 
Suppose a person mistakes a rope for a snake, he is told that it is not a snake but a rope. One word (snake) negates itself and reveals the other. This is "badhyam samaanaadhikaranya". 
Idam sarvam brahm - idam sarvam is negated - this is to be understood as badhyam samaanaadhikaranya.  mundak upanishad part 2, page २२

Explaination: 

"Sarvam khalu idam brahm" expresses the Vedantic concept that everything is indeed Brahman. Here, "idam" (this) and "sarvam" (everything) are in "samaanaadhikaranya," a grammatical term indicating they refer to the same object. It's similar to saying "Devadatta is a grammarian," where "Devadatta" and "grammarian" point to the same person.

In the example of mistaking a rope for a snake, when corrected, the word "snake" negates itself and reveals the truth of the object as a rope. This is termed "badhyam samaanaadhikaranya," where one word negates itself and points to another.

Applying this to "idam sarvam brahm," the phrase "idam sarvam" (all this) is negated, indicating that everything is Brahman. This negation operates similarly to the snake being negated to reveal the rope. It's a way of understanding that all that is perceived ultimately points to the singular reality of Brahman.
